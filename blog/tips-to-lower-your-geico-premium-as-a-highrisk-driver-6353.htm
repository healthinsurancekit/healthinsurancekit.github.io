Tips to Lower Your GEICO Premium as a High-Risk Driver

Home / Blog / Blog Details

Being labeled a high-risk driver can feel like a financial death sentence, especially when you see your GEICO premium skyrocketing. Whether it’s due to a DUI, multiple speeding tickets, or a lapse in coverage, insurance companies see you as a liability—and they charge accordingly. But don’t despair. Even as a high-risk driver, there are proven strategies to reduce your GEICO premium without sacrificing coverage.

Why High-Risk Drivers Pay More

Insurance companies like GEICO assess risk based on statistical data. If you’ve had accidents, traffic violations, or a history of missed payments, you’re statistically more likely to file a claim. This means higher premiums. Other factors that can classify you as high-risk include:

  • Young age (under 25) – Less driving experience equals higher risk.
  • Poor credit score – Studies show a correlation between low credit and increased claims.
  • Driving a high-performance vehicle – Faster cars often mean costlier repairs.
  • Living in a high-crime area – More theft and vandalism risks.

Understanding why you’re classified as high-risk is the first step toward lowering your premium.

Strategies to Reduce Your GEICO Premium

1. Take a Defensive Driving Course

Many states allow insurance discounts for completing an approved defensive driving course. These courses teach accident-avoidance techniques and can sometimes remove points from your driving record. GEICO often provides discounts of up to 10% for completing one.

Pro Tip: Check if your state mandates a specific course. Online options are usually cheaper and more flexible.

2. Improve Your Credit Score

Believe it or not, your credit score affects your insurance rate. GEICO, like most insurers, uses credit-based insurance scores to predict risk. Improving your credit can lead to significant savings.

How to boost your score quickly:
- Pay bills on time.
- Reduce credit card balances.
- Avoid opening new credit accounts unnecessarily.

3. Increase Your Deductible

A higher deductible means lower monthly premiums. If you’re a safe driver who rarely files claims, this can be a smart move. Just ensure you have enough savings to cover the deductible in case of an accident.

Example: Raising your deductible from $500 to $1,000 could save you 15-30% on premiums.

4. Bundle Your Insurance Policies

GEICO offers multi-policy discounts. Bundling auto insurance with renters, homeowners, or motorcycle insurance can lead to substantial savings.

Bonus: Some employers and organizations (like alumni associations) also offer group discounts through GEICO.

5. Drive Less (Or Switch to Usage-Based Insurance)

If you’ve transitioned to remote work or don’t drive much, GEICO’s DriveEasy program could save you money. This usage-based insurance tracks your driving habits (mileage, braking, speed) and adjusts rates accordingly.

Ideal for: Low-mileage drivers who avoid hard stops and speeding.

6. Remove Unnecessary Coverage

If you drive an older car, consider dropping collision and comprehensive coverage. If your car’s value is low, paying for full coverage may not be worth it.

Rule of thumb: If your car’s value is less than 10 times the annual premium for full coverage, it might be time to adjust.

7. Shop Around (But Stay Loyal When It Makes Sense)

High-risk drivers often assume they’re stuck with their current insurer. Not true. Some companies specialize in high-risk policies and may offer better rates.

When to stay with GEICO:
- If you’re close to having violations fall off your record.
- If GEICO offers accident forgiveness.

When to switch:
- If another insurer offers a significantly lower rate.
- If you find a company that specializes in high-risk drivers.

8. Add a Low-Risk Driver to Your Policy

If possible, adding a spouse or family member with a clean driving record to your policy can help lower rates. Just ensure they’re listed as a primary driver if they use the car frequently.

9. Avoid Small Claims

Filing multiple small claims can label you as a high-risk driver. If the repair cost is only slightly above your deductible, consider paying out of pocket to avoid premium hikes.

10. Monitor Your Driving Record

Errors on your driving record can unnecessarily inflate premiums. Request a copy of your record from the DMV and dispute any inaccuracies.

Long-Term Solutions for High-Risk Drivers

While quick fixes help, the best way to lower premiums is to improve your driving habits. Over time, violations and accidents will fall off your record, and insurers will see you as less of a risk.

Key milestones:
- 3 years: Most minor violations stop affecting rates.
- 5-7 years: Major incidents (like DUIs) may no longer impact premiums.

Patience and consistency pay off. In the meantime, use these strategies to keep your GEICO premium as low as possible.
